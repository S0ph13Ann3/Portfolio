project overview

In rural Rwanda, health facilities frequently run out of medicine due to poor communication and slow delivery coordination.

Patients travel long distances only to find that their required medication is unavailable.

CareRoute is a visual coordination system designed to address this. It enables district health managers to track medicine inventory, receive stock requests, and coordinate deliveries using existing transportation networks.

This project was designed to directly address key targets within UN SDG Goal 3: Good Health & Well-being.

The problem:

The core issue is not that the country lacks medicine, but that the people responsible for managing it don't have the right tools to communicate, track, or share real-time inventory data.

The system relies on handwritten notes, calls, and memory.

The results are:

  • Missed treatments and miscommunication.
  • Overloaded workers with no visibility into stock levels.
  • Medicines lost, delayed, or wasted.

user research

Our process was guided by the principle: we are not the user. We developed two primary personas based on insights from sources like VillageReach and the Global Health Supply Chain Summit.

User context matters:

Unlike digital-first users, our stakeholders operate in low-bandwidth, high-pressure, and often low-literacy environments.

  • They rely heavily on analog tools like notebooks and SMS.
  • Many use basic phones or budget Android devices.
  • They juggle high responsibility with minimal support.

Patrice Uwimana

Age: 28
Role & Location: A Community Health Worker (CHW) in the rural Nyamagabe District.

Frustrations: Long travel, paper logs, and missed deliveries.

Goals: Log and report inventory quickly, reduce walking, and spend more time with patients.

Jean Bosco Nshimiyimana

Age: 41
Role & Location: A District Supply Coordinator in Butare Town, managing 20+ rural clinics.

Frustrations: Poor visibility into clinic stock, an over-reliance on calls and paper, and information overload.

Goals: Prioritize routes, prevent stockouts, and generate clean reports.

Why this matters for design:

Understanding these personas guided every part of our product design:

  • Jean's dashboard is fast, lightweight, and shows urgent issues first.
  • Patrice's mobile app works offline and caches data to reduce stress.

By designing for real constraints, not ideal ones, we ensure CareRoute meets users where they are — not where we wish they were.

the solution

CareRoute is a lightweight, dual-platform (mobile + web) coordination tool designed for rural health workers managing medicine delivery under pressure.

It is not a high-tech overhaul. Instead, it embraces the hybrid reality of rural health workers, bridging the gap between paper-based operations and digital coordination.

For Beatrice (Mobile): A simple interface to request, confirm, and track medicine deliveries with offline-first functionality.

For Jean Bosco (Web): A dashboard view of urgent stockouts and delivery assignments to support faster decisions with fewer errors.

Mobile flow:

App Launches → Offline Sync Alert: The app prompts: "Weak signal detected. Load latest saved data?" This gives her a sense of control.

Land on Stock Tab: The default view shows inventory cards with a clear status: ✅ Full / 🟡 Low / 🔴 Empty.

Tap on a Low Stock Item: This opens a detail screen with a CTA at the bottom: [Request Delivery].

Request Delivery Screen: A simple form auto-fills the medicine name. She only needs to input the quantity and an optional note. The app auto-queues the request to sync if offline.

Confirmation: She receives a success message: "Delivery request sent." or, if offline, "Saved. Will send when connection is restored".

Track Delivery Status: She can track the request as its status updates from ⏳ "Awaiting assignment" to 🚗 "Out for delivery".

Confirm Delivery: Upon arrival, she inputs a 4-digit code from the driver and taps [Confirm Receipt] to close the loop.

Web flow:

Urgent Requests (Top): The top panel is a triage area, displaying the most urgent stock requests as actionable cards so critical needs are addressed first.

Insights & Tasks (Middle): The middle panel provides actionable insights, pairing data visualizations (like request frequency) with a clear task list of pending approvals and unconfirmed deliveries.

System Logs (Bottom): The bottom panel features a system log of all recent activity, offering a transparent and documented record to build user confidence.

future vision & learnings

Our strategy is to start with a controlled pilot in 1-2 rural clinics, then validate features under real-world conditions before scaling regionally.

Future vision:

  • Emotional Wellness Layer: Add a "mood check" emoji system to help supervisors track burnout.
  • Analytics Dashboard: Enable data exports to funders and reveal trends in medicine waste and delivery delays.
  • Expansion Playbook: Create a "plug-and-play" model to localize for similar contexts in Malawi, Tanzania, or Uganda.

Learnings:

  • Tech ≠ Transformation: Tools only work if they're designed with empathy for real-world users.
  • Designing for constraint is power: Limited signal and time aren't barriers—they're design signals that lead to focused solutions.
  • User research is our compass: Insights from field reports and user interviews kept us grounded in reality, not theory.
