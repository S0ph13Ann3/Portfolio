project overview

College is marketed as the "best years of your life," yet for many women, it’s isolating. Quilly isn't just an app; it's a digital sisterhood.
The Problem: College women lack a safe, authentic digital space to find community, often feeling lonely despite being constantly connected.

The Goal: Design a femme-centered "pocket guide" that prioritizes real-world connection, safety, and genuine support over vanity metrics.

The Impact: A successfully launched beta at UC Berkeley that helps students find their "forever friends" and navigate college with less stress.

context

The challenge & the goal:

The rise of loneliness among college women has risen over the years and Quilly has made it our mission to create a real, supportive community — not just another social app or club. It offers a safe, authentic space where students can form meaningful connections and get the everyday support they need to feel less alone.

My role:

I joined Quilly as a UX Designer, leading the design efforts for the beta version of the app. My work involved integrating user research conducted prior to my arrival and closely partnering with the CEO to bring their design vision to life in a way that aligned with user needs and product goals.

Initial Screens:

In our first design iteration, we used house colors as the primary theme throughout the app, based on each user's assigned house, with Quilly's brand colors serving as accents. While this added a fun, personalized touch, the house colors often felt overpowering and didn't align with the clean, minimal aesthetic we were aiming for.

During this phase, we were also finalizing which features would be included in the beta launch. As a result, many of our early designs explored different navigation bar layouts, with icons varying depending on the feature set we were considering at the time.

First Re-design:

After user feedback, we realized many comments were about the color scheme. We decided to tone down the house colors and go for a more minimal and clean look. Finalizing the main beta features also helped us create a more consistent and user-friendly nav bar.

We also discussed adding a feature similar to Instagram's "Close Friends," where users can see what their besties are up to.

Second Re-design:

When presenting our initial design to the CEO, she noted that while we had achieved a more subtle color scheme, it still lacked the clean, fresh aesthetic she envisioned.

This feedback sent us back to the drawing board, where our focus shifted to creating a truly simple, intuitive UI that prioritizes ample white space, clear information hierarchy, and an effortlessly user-friendly layout.

This became the official beta version we handed off to our software engineers.

what's next?

Our beta is now live for students at UC Berkeley, available for download on our website. This initial launch is a key step as we refine the app based on user feedback, with plans to expand our community to more universities soon.
