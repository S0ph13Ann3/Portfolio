App Prototype

project overview

Google Maps is one of the top leading navigation tool in the world, but its core algorithm is optimized for efficiency. It frequently fails at finding the safest or most accessible one. It often suggests unlit alleys, parks at night, or routes with physical barriers (like boulders or stairs) for wheelchair users.

This case study takes this concern and showcases a solution that users on Google Maps can use to not only efficiently get to their destination but in a safe manner as well.

The Goal: To transform Google Maps from a tool that optimizes for time into a tool that optimizes for peace of mind.

discovery & research

The shortcut that felt like a wrong turn…

In 2025, my sister and I were navigating Tokyo at night. Google Maps promised us a 4-minute shortcut back to the hotel. That 'shortcut' led us away from the main road and into a series of pitch-black, narrow alleys with no streetlights or open shops.

This experience made me stop and think: Why does the world's most powerful navigation tool prioritize speed over personal security?

I began searching through the settings for a 'Safety' toggle, only to find it didn't exist for pedestrians.

That night in Tokyo led me to ask: What if we could toggle SAFETY like we toggle 'Avoid Tolls'?

Intial Research:

Google Maps is excellent for "point-to-point" navigation, however, its algorithms often ignore environmental factors like lighting and visibility, leading users into "sketchy" or uncomfortable areas.
Current navigation algorithms are built on a "Logic of Efficiency" (Time + Distance). However, recent data shows that for many users, efficiency is secondary to safety and visibility .

The "Walking Fear" Data (2025)

A national survey commissioned in August 2025 revealed a staggering gap in how different demographics experience navigation:

"67% of women identified walking alone at night as their primary safety fear—significantly outpacing traveling in unfamiliar areas... 38% are forced to take precautionary measures every single day to feel secure."

— Security Today, "Walking Alone at Night is the Greatest Safety Fear," Aug 2025

Direct User Requests (2025–2026)

On public forums and community boards, users have been vocal about the lack of "Safe Routing" options:

"Imagine it is late in the night and you have to walk home alone in a city you don't know... there should be an option to avoid small streets and other places where you could get mugged or worse."

My own research confirmed that my experience in the alleys of Japan wasn't an isolated incident; it's a recurring pain point for urban navigators using Google Maps.

Users are explicitly asking for safety features, even if they result in slightly longer travel times. There is a clear market demand for a visibility-based layer in standard navigation apps.

personas & user flow

After conducting my own research, I realized that my experience in Japan was a widespread pain point, which allowed me to build data-driven personas that reflect the real safety needs of everyday users.

Elena

"I value safety over speed in unfamiliar places."

Context: Tourist navigating cities like Tokyo after dark.

Goal: Find well-lit, predictable routes to her hotel.

Frustration: Google Maps suggests "shortcuts" through pitch-black alleys to save 3 minutes.

Motivation: Predictability and visibility are more important than efficiency.

Marcus

"The map needs to know my city changes at night."

Context: Late-shift hospitality worker walking home at 11:00 PM.

Goal: Stick to high-traffic main roads with verified lighting.

Frustration: The app treats a 2:00 AM walk like a 2:00 PM walk, routing him through dark parks.

Motivation: Wants a dynamic map and a way to report broken streetlights to help others.

User Flow:

By mapping the specific frustrations of Elena and Marcus against the existing Google Maps architecture, I developed a user flow that integrates safety naturally into the current navigation and contribution tabs.
